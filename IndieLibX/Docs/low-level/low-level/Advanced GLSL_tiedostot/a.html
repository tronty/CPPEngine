                        I am so sorry, for some weird reason I didn't consider your comment to be about uniform buffer objects (which makes a lot sense in the context of this tutorial) and assumed you were talking about vertex buffer objects, sorry!

                        You can completely disregard my previous comment (I will delete it to avoid further confusion).

                        Given that I now understand your comment, yes; if you are changing the buffer every frame it's probably more wise to use something like GL_DYNAMIC_DRAW to tell OpenGL to put it in a better memory configuration for frequent changes.                                 JoeyDeVries Sensei Hello World • 2 years ago

                                They both change the point size. If you haven't enabled GL_PROGRAM_POINT_SIZE with 

                                glEnable(GL_PROGRAM_POINT_SIZE);

                                then OpenGL uses the point size from glPointSize.

                                If you did enable GL_PROGRAM_POINT_SIZE then it uses the point size as written by gl_PointSize. So based on whether GL_PROGRAM_POINT_SIZE is enabled you need to use either one of the two approaches.

                                                                                                  JoeyDeVries Sensei m_pNullptr • 2 years ago

                                                                                                  I'm not an expert on water rendering but I don't think using gl_FragCoord would work (unless you can come up with a creative solution) since you want the reflection to map to the water surface such that the texture also looks as if it's oriented on top of the water surface (with rotation and perspective). If you simply use gl_FragCoord the reflection will always look as if you're looking directly into the water (from the top) which I think will look fake.

                                                                                                  What most people do (and I did once as well) is to use the normalized device coordinates (scaled to the [0, 1] interval range) of the water surface as the texture coordinates. Here is a snippet of one of my shaders:

                                                                                                  vec4 projCoord = mvpPos / mvpPos.w;
                                                                                                  projCoord += vec4(1.0);
                                                                                                  projCoord *= 0.5;
                                                                                                  projCoord = clamp(projCoord, 0.0, 1.0);

                                                                                                  Where mvpPos is the water's FragPos multiplied by modelViewProjection matrix. I then use this projCoord.xy to sample from the reflection/refraction textures.

                                                                                                  People also often offset this texture coordinate slightly by using a DUDV map.
